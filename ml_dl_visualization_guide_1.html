What is Machine Learning and Deep Learning?
🧒
What is Machine Learning (ML)?
Traditional programming works like a recipe: you write exact rules, and the computer follows them.

Machine Learning is different. Instead of writing rules, you show the computer thousands of examples and let it figure out the rules on its own.
Everyday analogy: Think of teaching a child what a dog is. You don't describe all the rules of "dog-ness." You just show them hundreds of pictures of dogs and say "dog." Eventually, the child can spot a dog they've never seen before. ML works the same way.
🧠
What is Deep Learning (DL)?
Deep Learning is a special type of Machine Learning. It uses neural networks — layers of mathematical functions loosely inspired by neurons in the human brain.

"Deep" just means the network has many layers stacked on top of each other. Each layer learns increasingly complex patterns.
Everyday analogy: The first layer of a vision network learns to detect edges. The next layer combines edges into shapes. The next combines shapes into faces. Each layer builds on the last — like going from letters → words → sentences → meaning.
🤖
Why Does This Power ChatGPT, DALL·E, and More?
ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini are all built on deep learning. Specifically, they use a type of neural network called a Transformer.

DALL·E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion use a type called a Diffusion Model. These were trained on billions of image-text pairs to learn what things look like.
What "trained" means: Training is the process where the model sees millions of examples, makes predictions, compares them to correct answers, and adjusts its internal numbers to do better. Like practicing a skill over and over until you get good at it.

// Plain-English Glossary
Every Scary Word, Explained Simply

This guide uses technical words because they're precise — but every one of them has a simple meaning. Bookmark this section and come back whenever you see an unfamiliar term.

Neural Network
A math system made of layers of "neurons" (numbers) that learn to recognize patterns. Inspired loosely by how the brain connects neurons. Not actually biological — it's just math.
Training
The process of feeding a model thousands of examples so it can adjust its internal numbers (called weights) to get better at a task. Like practicing a sport by doing it over and over.
Weights / Parameters
The internal numbers inside a neural network that get adjusted during training. GPT-4 has ~1.7 trillion parameters. Think of them as dials the model tunes to improve its predictions.
Loss / Error
A number that measures how wrong the model's prediction was. During training, the goal is to make the loss as small as possible. Lower loss = better performance.
Gradient Descent
The algorithm that adjusts the model's weights to reduce loss. It moves in the direction that makes the error go down, like rolling a ball downhill on a landscape to find the lowest point.
Backpropagation
The method for calculating how much each weight contributed to the error, so gradient descent knows what to adjust. It works backwards through the network — hence "back" propagation.
CNN (Convolutional Neural Network)
A type of neural network designed to understand images. It scans the image in small patches (using "kernels") to detect edges, textures, and shapes. Powers face recognition, medical scans, self-driving cars.
Transformer
The architecture behind ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and BERT. It reads all words at once (unlike older models) and uses "attention" to understand which words relate to which others in a sentence.
Attention Mechanism
A way for the model to focus on the most relevant parts of its input. In the sentence "The cat sat because it was tired," attention helps the model link "it" back to "cat." Without attention, LLMs couldn't exist.
LLM (Large Language Model)
A very large Transformer trained on massive amounts of text. "Large" means billions of parameters. Examples: GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama. They predict the next word — and doing that very well creates the illusion of understanding.
GAN (Generative Adversarial Network)
Two networks competing: a Generator tries to create fake data, a Discriminator tries to detect fakes. They improve each other. GANs can generate realistic faces, art, and more.
Diffusion Model
Learns to remove noise from a randomly noisy image step by step. At generation time it starts from pure noise and progressively "denoises" it into an image. Powers Stable Diffusion, DALL·E 3, Midjourney.
Overfitting
When a model memorizes the training data instead of learning general patterns. Like a student who memorizes answers instead of understanding the material — fails on new questions they haven't seen.
Feature Map / Activation
The output of a layer in a neural network — a grid of numbers representing what patterns the layer detected in the input. Early layers detect edges. Later layers detect faces, objects, concepts.
Embedding
A way to represent words, images, or any data as a list of numbers. Similar things get similar number lists. The word "king" and "queen" have embeddings close to each other in mathematical space.
t-SNE / UMAP
Techniques for visualizing high-dimensional data in 2D. Because embeddings have hundreds of dimensions, you can't see them directly — these tools squish them into 2D plots you can actually look at.

// What is a Neural Network? (Plain English)

A neural network is a mathematical system loosely modeled on the brain. It consists of layers of "neurons" (just numbers) connected to each other. When you feed it an input — say, an image or a sentence — the numbers flow through the layers, getting transformed at each step, until the final layer produces an output (a prediction).

What makes neural networks powerful is learning. Each connection between neurons has a "weight" — a number that controls how strongly one neuron influences the next. During training, these weights are automatically adjusted millions of times until the network gets good at its task.

Why does depth matter? Shallow networks can only learn simple patterns. Deep networks (many layers) can learn incredibly complex patterns — because each layer builds on what previous layers learned. Layer 1 might learn edges. Layer 5 might learn faces. Layer 20 might learn the concept of "a smiling face in sunglasses."

// What is a CNN? (Plain English)

A CNN (Convolutional Neural Network) is a type of neural network specifically designed to understand images. Regular neural networks struggle with images because a 256×256 pixel image has 65,536 numbers — and a regular network would need to look at all of them at once in a very inefficient way.

CNNs solve this by using a technique called convolution: instead of looking at the whole image at once, a small filter (called a "kernel") slides across the image patch by patch, detecting specific patterns — like edges going left-right, or diagonal, or curved. This is much more efficient and learns "position-independent" features (an eye is an eye whether it's in the top-left or bottom-right of the image).

Where are CNNs used? Face recognition on your phone. Medical X-ray analysis. Self-driving car lane detection. Google Image Search. Instagram filters. Any time a computer "sees" something, a CNN is usually involved.

// What is a Transformer? (Plain English)

A Transformer is the AI architecture behind ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, BERT, and virtually every modern AI language model. It was introduced in a famous 2017 paper titled "Attention Is All You Need" — and it changed everything.

The key idea is attention. Previous language models read text word by word, left to right, like a human reading. Transformers read the entire sentence at once and figure out which words are most relevant to each other. In "The cat sat on the mat because it was tired," attention helps the model understand that "it" refers to "cat" — even though they're far apart.

Why are LLMs (Large Language Models) so capable? Because the Transformer architecture can be scaled up enormously. Train a large enough Transformer on enough text (basically the entire internet), and it develops surprisingly deep abilities — translation, coding, reasoning, writing. GPT-4 has roughly 1.7 trillion parameters. The original 2017 Transformer had 65 million.

🎨 Generative AI
3 Tools for GANs & Diffusion

Generative models create new things — images, audio, video. These tools make the training dynamics visible.

// What are Generative AI Models? (Plain English)

Generative AI refers to models that can create new content — images, text, music, video — rather than just classify or analyze existing content. There are two main families:

GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks) use a "competition" approach. Two networks fight each other: a Generator that creates fake images, and a Discriminator that tries to detect fakes. As they compete, the Generator gets better and better at making realistic images. The faces on thispersondoesnotexist.com are generated by a GAN.

Diffusion Models use a "noise removal" approach. During training, the model learns to remove noise from images step by step. At generation time, you start with pure random noise and apply the denoising process repeatedly — and a realistic image emerges from the noise. Stable Diffusion, DALL·E 3, and Midjourney all use diffusion. This approach has largely replaced GANs for image generation because it's more stable and controllable.

G3 The Illustrated Stable Diffusion → OPEN
✓ Beginner Friendly ✓ No Coding ✓ Read This Before G2
Diffusion Visual Essay
Jay Alammar's visual companion to understanding latent diffusion models.
Read this before the Diffusion Explainer (G2). Jay Alammar applies his signature illustrated-essay style — the same approach that made the Illustrated Transformer so successful — to Stable Diffusion. Every step is explained with custom diagrams showing data shapes and flow.

What is a "latent space"? Stable Diffusion doesn't do its denoising on the full-resolution image (that would be too slow). Instead, it compresses the image into a smaller "latent" representation using a VAE (Variational Autoencoder). The denoising happens in this compressed space, and only at the end is the image decompressed back to full resolution. Alammar's diagrams make this crystal clear.

What is cross-attention for text? The U-Net doesn't just denoise randomly — your text prompt guides it at every step. Cross-attention lets the U-Net "look at" your text representation while deciding what noise to remove. This is why changing a single word in your prompt changes the whole image.

📐 Distill.pub
5 Must-Read Interactive Research Articles

Distill.pub is peer-reviewed, interactive, and built by the researchers who created the models being explained. The gold standard for deep ML understanding.

// What is Distill.pub?

Distill was a peer-reviewed scientific journal — but instead of dry PDFs, every article was an interactive webpage. Readers could move sliders, drag points, and manipulate visualizations to understand concepts directly. D1 How to Use t-SNE Effectively → OPEN

Dimensionality Reduction Interactive
Kills a dozen common t-SNE misconceptions with live sliders.
What is t-SNE? Neural networks represent data as lists of hundreds or thousands of numbers (called embeddings or vectors). You can't visualize a 512-dimensional space directly. t-SNE is an algorithm that squishes this high-dimensional data into a 2D plot you can actually see — while trying to keep similar things close together.

The problem: t-SNE is widely misunderstood. People see clusters in a t-SNE plot and assume they represent meaningful groups. But this article shows — with live sliders — that t-SNE can create clusters from random data, that cluster sizes mean nothing, that distances between clusters mean nothing. These are dangerous misconceptions that have led to wrong conclusions in published research.

Try this first: Move the "Perplexity" slider (the most important parameter) all the way down to 2, then to 100. Watch the visualization change completely. The same data, completely different-looking "clusters." This is the core lesson of the article.

D2 Why Momentum Really Works → OPEN
△ Some Math — Worth It ✓ Interactive Loss Landscapes
Optimization Interactive
The intuition for SGD, momentum, and Adam — on interactive loss landscapes.
What is optimization in ML? Training a neural network means finding the set of weights that minimizes the loss (error). This is done by a family of algorithms called "optimizers." The most basic is gradient descent — moving downhill on the loss landscape. More advanced optimizers (Momentum, Adam, AdamW) train faster and more reliably.

What is "momentum"? Imagine rolling a ball down a hill. Without momentum, the ball stops the moment the ground levels off, even if there's a deeper valley just ahead. With momentum, the ball keeps rolling through flat regions and small hills. Momentum in neural network training works the same way — it helps the optimizer not get stuck in shallow local minima.

The interactive part: Drag the starting point on the loss surface and watch gradient descent with and without momentum navigate toward the minimum. The difference is immediately obvious. This article builds the intuition that makes every optimizer choice (Adam vs SGD vs RMSProp) feel logical rather than arbitrary.

D3 Attention and Augmented RNNs → OPEN
△ Intermediate ✓ Historical Context for Attention
Attention Visual Essay
The foundational visual overview of attention — written before transformers took over.
Why read a 2016 article? Because understanding where attention came from makes the Transformer architecture feel like a natural evolution rather than a mysterious invention. This article was written by Chris Olah and Shan Carter at a time when attention was a new addition to existing language models called RNNs (Recurrent Neural Networks).

What is an RNN? An older type of neural network that processes sequences word by word, left to right, keeping a "memory" of what it has seen. The problem: the memory could only hold so much information. Long sentences caused important early words to be "forgotten." Attention was invented to fix this — letting the model directly access any earlier word, bypassing the memory bottleneck.

Why this matters today: The attention mechanism in this article is the same fundamental mechanism at the core of every Transformer. Reading this gives you the "why" behind the design that powers ChatGPT.

D4 Visualizing Neural Network Loss Landscapes → OPEN
△ Intermediate ✓ Visual — Easy to Follow
Neural Nets Optimization
What does the loss surface of a deep network actually look like?
What is a "loss landscape"? Imagine a neural network has millions of parameters (weights). If you could visualize the loss for every possible combination of weights, you'd get an incredibly high-dimensional "landscape" — some regions are mountains (high loss), some are valleys (low loss). Training is the process of finding a valley.

The problem visualizers use: You can't visualize millions of dimensions. Researchers project the high-dimensional landscape into 2D to see its shape. This article shows how that's done — and reveals something important: different neural network architectures create very different landscape shapes. Some are smooth and easy to optimize. Others are chaotic and full of sharp cliffs.

Key insight: Residual connections (the "skip connections" in ResNets) dramatically smooth the loss landscape — which is why ResNets train so much better than plain deep networks. The article shows this visually.
